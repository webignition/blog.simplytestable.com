--- layout: default title: "Accepting Discount Codes Without Distraction or Disappointment" date: 2014-08-20 14:30:00 author: name: Jon Cram url: https://github.com/webignition ---

I'm preparing to run ads for Simply Testable that feature coupon codes offering discounts.

I needed to build in the ability to accept discount codes. This, as always, required a spot of upfront learning and a careful redesign of the account creation pages.

The most obvious solution - to add a 'discount code' field to the sign up page - is non-ideal through being distracting and disappointing.

Here's how I worked my way to finding a better solution.

Easy, just add a 'discount code' field

I've used the Internet a bit for the past few years. I know how these things work.

If you want to allow people to use discount codes, you add a 'discount code' field to your sign up page:

Someone hears your ad on a podcast, jots down the discount code, rushes to your site and enters the discount code when creating an account.

Not that easy

One quick new field later and … tada! … profit! Converting new users into paying users has never been so easy.

Except the user has to make sure to jot down or remember the discount code. And the user has to enter the discount code. Correctly, without transposing any characters.

Except that this is one more field for the user to fill in. Adding a third user-fillable field to a form having only two user-fillable fields is a big increase. A bump in cognitive load is not unexpected.

And users without a discount code are abruptly struck in the face. Discount code? I can get this more cheaply? Others are getting this more cheaply and I am not? Why don't I have a discount code? Where can I get a discount code?

For users without a discount code, focus can shift quickly away from creating an account to resolving the need to acquire a price reduction.

Distraction (I'll just hunt down a discount code, how hard can that be?)

I'll go to Google, enter [Simply Testable discount code] and I'll be on my way.

Oh, here we go. First result looks promising, let's see what that is. Nope, no discount code, just a blog post about accepting discount codes without distraction or disappointment. Too meta.

Ok, the second result should surely do it. Nope, that's a page in simplified Chinese that happens to mention both [Simply Testable] and [discount code] but not in a related context. Surprising amounts of blinking text but not what I was looking for.

Hmm, how about I change my search terms a bit. That'll work better with a cup of tea, let me sort that out first.

… 10 minutes pass …

Off to the afternoon project planning meeting. I've still got half a cup of coffee left which is nice.

…

Distraction. The user is no longer focused on creating an account. Add to a form in any way that encourages the user to leave your site and you reduce the chance of converting that user into a paying customer.

Disappointment (I guess I'll have to pay the full price)

You've been using a SaaS product for free, perhaps a limited demo, and you've decided that the value provided by the service is worth exchanging for some of your cash.

You're at the sign up page and you're sure that £19 per month is a fair exchange for what the service can do for you. You're happy to pay.

Ooh, I can enter a discount code? I'll go to Google, enter [Simply Testable discount code] and I'll be on my way.

…

You can't find a discount code. You mentally block out the discount code field and begrudgingly decide to pay the full price.

Just minutes ago you were happy to pay. The price asked for the value provided seemed fair. You still need to pay but you're not as happy to do so.

You now feel that the service could have been bought at a lower monthly price. You're being charged more than you should be. Regardless of the fact that just minutes ago you were happy to pay the price being asked.

Some people, on not being able to find a discount code, will change their mind. Others will continue but reluctantly. Some will continue with as much enthusiasm as before.

What you don't get is all new users proceeding to create an account and of the ones that do, not all are as happy to do so.

Accepting discount codes with no discount code field

The addition of a discount field is non-ideal. It leads to increased cognitive load, distraction and disappointment.

We need a way of accepting discount codes without having a discount code field.

Thankfully there is a simple way to achieve this: don't promote discount codes and instead promote landing page URLs.

Don't encourage users to go to example.com and enter discount code "foo" for 20% off their order. Encourage users to go to example.com/foo for 20% off their order.

After all, every good promotion deserves a good (or at least unique) landing page, so why not take advantage of what you can do when users reach your landing page?

The landing page can set a cookie that holds the discount code. The page that would otherwise include a discount code field reads the cookie and clearly informs the user of the discount they are getting.

The bad process (using a discount code field):

  • Encourage user to visit example.com and use discount code "foo" for 20% off

  • User visits example.com and, in time, proceeds to the checkout or sign up page

  • User enters discount code "foo"

  • User is informed that they now get 20% off their order

The good process (using a discount code cookie):

  • Encourage user to visit example.com/foo for 20% off

  • User visits example.com/foo and, in time, proceeds to the checkout or sign up page

  • User is informed that they now get 20% off their order
